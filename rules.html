WAR. WAR NEVER CHANGES.

Fallout 2D20 is a tabletop roleplaying game set in the post-apocalyptic world of the Fallout franchise, published by Modiphius Entertainment. It uses the 2D20 system, a dice pool mechanic where players roll handfuls of twenty-sided dice to determine outcomes, generating narrative-driving resources called Momentum and facing a countervailing threat from the Action Point pool.

What You'll Need

Each player needs 2‚Äì3 twenty-sided dice (d20) and a supply of Combat Dice (d6). The game master typically keeps extra sets. You'll also want tokens or beads to track Momentum and AP, and character sheets.

The Core Fantasy

Players are survivors carving out existence in a devastated America circa 2287 (or another Fallout era). Sessions involve scavenging, faction politics, exploration, moral choices, and occasionally very violent confrontations.

The 2D20 Loop

Every significant action in Fallout 2D20 follows the same fundamental structure:

Core Loop

1. Declare action and select a Skill
2. Set a Difficulty (0‚Äì5 Complications Tokens needed)
3. Roll 2d20 (buy up to 3 extra d20s with Momentum/Luck)
4. Count successes ‚Äî each die ‚â§ your Skill + Attribute target number
5. Generate Momentum from excess successes or suffer Complications from 20s

Key Resources

‚ö° Momentum

A shared party pool of bonus successes. Spend to buy extra dice, increase damage, create advantages. Max 6 Momentum at end of turn.

‚ò¢ Action Points (AP)

The GM's counter-resource. Fuels enemy actions and complications. Players generate AP when failing or rolling complications. GM starts with 1 AP per player.

üçÄ Luck

Personal points derived from your Luck attribute. Spend as Momentum (personal use only). Slow to recover ‚Äî rest or use a Perk.

Sessions of Play

A typical Fallout 2D20 session sees players navigating Scenes ‚Äî each scene representing a distinct location or encounter. The GM describes the environment, characters make choices and roll dice when uncertain outcomes matter. Between scenes, the party may rest, trade, or plan. The Wasteland is as much about roleplaying faction allegiances and moral ambiguity as it is about gunfights with Raiders.

New to 2D20?

Start with the Core Rules section to understand dice tests, then move to Combat. The S.P.E.C.I.A.L. and Skills sections define your character's mechanical identity.

S.P.E.C.I.A.L.
// PRIMARY ATTRIBUTE SYSTEM ‚Äî SEVEN FOUNDATIONAL STATISTICS //

Every character in Fallout 2D20 is defined by seven core attributes, collectively known as S.P.E.C.I.A.L. These attributes range from 4 to 12 (default 7; 5 points to distribute at character creation). They serve as the target number added to a Skill rank when determining test difficulty targets.

The Formula

Target Number (TN) = Attribute + Skill Rank
Roll 2d20 against this number. Each die that rolls equal to or under the TN scores one success. Difficulty sets how many successes you need.

Attribute Checks Without a Skill

When no skill directly applies, the GM may call for a raw Attribute test. The Target Number is simply the Attribute value alone, and the player rolls 2d20 against that number at Difficulty 1 (or higher for harder challenges).

The Test

Whenever a character attempts something with meaningful risk and uncertain outcome, the GM calls for a test. Routine tasks with no real failure consequences don't require rolls.

Step-by-Step Test Resolution

1. State Intent ‚Äî Describe what you're trying to do.
2. Choose Skill ‚Äî GM approves the relevant skill.
3. Set Difficulty (0‚Äì5) ‚Äî Number of successes needed.
4. Roll Pool ‚Äî Start with 2d20; buy up to 3 extra d20s with Momentum or Luck (1 per die).

7. Resolve Outcome ‚Äî Successes ‚â• Difficulty = success. Extra successes become Momentum.

Momentum Cap

The party Momentum pool has a maximum of 6 points at any time. Excess Momentum is lost at end of a player's turn. Spend it or lose it!

Action Points (AP)

Action Points are the Game Master's resource, the dark mirror of Momentum. The GM uses AP to make enemies more dangerous and reactive.

How the GM Gains AP

‚Ä¢ Players generate Complications (rolling 20s)
‚Ä¢ Players purchase extra dice with Momentum
‚Ä¢ GM starts each scene with 1 AP per player
‚Ä¢ Some NPCs and encounters grant bonus AP

How the GM Spends AP

‚Ä¢ Give NPCs extra actions (1 AP each)
‚Ä¢ Introduce environmental hazards (1‚Äì2 AP)
‚Ä¢ Have enemies act earlier in initiative (1 AP)
‚Ä¢ Trigger Complications as narrative events (varies)

Luck

Personal to each character, Luck points can be spent exactly like Momentum ‚Äî but only for that character's own rolls. They cannot be pooled or shared. Luck points refresh after a Major Rest (overnight) or via certain Perks.

Focus and Complications

üéØ Focus (Critical Success)

When a d20 rolls equal to or under your Skill Rank alone (not the full TN), it generates 2 successes instead of 1. Characters with high skill ranks generate criticals more often, representing deep expertise.

üí• Complications

Any die rolling exactly 20 is a Complication. The GM gains 1 AP and may narrate a narrative complication ‚Äî a gun jams, you slip, an NPC notices you. The test can still succeed with complications.

Opposed Tests

When two characters directly contest each other (sneaking past a guard, arm wrestling, fast-drawing), both roll tests simultaneously. The character with more successes wins. Ties go to the active character (whoever initiated the contest). Ties can also be broken by Luck ‚Äî higher attribute breaks the tie.

COMBAT
// VIOLENCE IS RARELY THE ANSWER ‚Äî BUT IT'S USUALLY AN ANSWER //

Initiative

At the start of combat, each participant rolls an Initiative test: roll 2d20 against Agility + Perception (Difficulty 1). Count successes ‚Äî higher successes go first. Ties are broken by the Agility + Perception total, then by Luck attribute.

Turn Order

Play alternates: one player character acts, then one enemy acts (GM spends 1 AP per extra enemy action). This continues until all characters have acted, ending the Round. A new Round begins with any leftover Momentum reduced by 1.

Action Economy

On your turn, you have Action Points (NOT the GM's AP ‚Äî these are personal combat actions) based on Agility. Most characters get:

Major Actions (1 per turn)

Make an Attack, Sprint (Agility √ó 2 yards), Use an Item (Stimpak, etc.), Aim (reduce difficulty of next attack), Help an ally.

Minor Actions (1 per turn)

Draw or holster a weapon, Reload, Open a door, Take Cover, move up to Agility yards (standard move).

Extra Actions

Spend 2 Momentum to gain an extra Minor Action. The AP resource (GM's) is different from personal combat actions ‚Äî don't confuse them!

Making an Attack

Attack Sequence

1. Declare target and weapon
2. Determine base Difficulty (usually 1; modified by range, cover, etc.)
3. Roll Skill test (Guns, Melee, etc.) against TN
4. If successes ‚â• Difficulty: Hit! Roll damage dice (Combat Dice)
5. Apply target's Physical/Energy Resistance
6. Remaining damage reduces target's HP

Damage & Combat Dice

Damage is rolled with Combat Dice (CD) ‚Äî a custom d6 with values: blank (0), 1, 1, 2, 2, Effect ‚òÖ. The Effect face triggers a weapon's special effect (ammo type, critical injury, etc.).

Physical Resistance (PR)

Comes from armor. Subtract PR from total damage after dice are rolled. Damage reduced to 0 deals no harm, though Effects may still trigger.

Energy Resistance (ER)

Applies specifically to energy weapon damage. Heavy metal armor has high PR; lighter suits may have better ER. Radiation damage bypasses most resistances.

Critical Injuries

When a character reaches 0 HP and still takes damage, roll on the Critical Injury table using the excess damage as a modifier. Injuries range from a Concussion to Gruesome Death and may require Medicine rolls to stabilize.

Cover & Concealment

Cover (Hard)

Walls, cars, concrete barriers. Attackers must spend 1 success to bypass cover before damage can be applied. Cover has its own HP and can be destroyed.

Concealment (Soft)

Smoke, darkness, bushes. Adds +1 difficulty to attacks against the concealed character. Does not stop damage once hit.

Conditions

BLINDED ‚ñ∂
All visual Perception tests and ranged attacks suffer +2 difficulty. Cannot perform aimed attacks. Moving is halved without assistance.
PRONE ‚ñ∂
Standing up costs a Minor Action. Melee attackers gain +1 difficulty to hit a prone target. Ranged attacks against prone targets also gain +1 difficulty at Close range and beyond; attackers at Reach have easier time (+0). Shooting while prone gives +1 die for Aimed attacks.
SLOWED ‚ñ∂
Movement is halved (round down). Cannot Sprint. Usually caused by heavy encumbrance, leg injury, or difficult terrain.
STUNNED ‚ñ∂
Cannot take Major Actions this turn. Minor Actions still permitted. Stunned characters cannot take Reactions. Typically lasts 1 round unless otherwise specified.
POISONED ‚ñ∂
Suffer 1 damage (ignoring Resistance) at the start of each of your turns. Medicine test (D2) removes the condition. RadAway may help with radiation-based poisons.
IRRADIATED ‚ñ∂
Track Radiation separately as a secondary HP pool (Rads). Accumulated Rads reduce max HP by 1 per 5 Rads. At max Rads = max HP value, the character dies. RadAway reduces Rads; Rad Resistance reduces incoming radiation damage.

PERKS & TRAITS
// CHARACTER CUSTOMIZATION ‚Äî SPECIAL ABILITIES AND BACKGROUND FEATURES //

Perks are special abilities that give characters unique mechanical advantages. Each Perk has a Rank requirement (tied to a skill or attribute threshold) and some have multiple tiers. Characters receive Perks through leveling up or as rewards for significant in-game achievements.

Gaining Perks

Characters begin with 1 Perk at creation and gain additional Perks as they accumulate Experience Points (XP). Perks are purchased at Perk Thresholds ‚Äî typically every 1,000 XP. Prerequisites (skill ranks, attribute minimums) must be met.

Traits vs. Perks

Traits are selected at character creation and represent intrinsic qualities ‚Äî they always come with both a benefit AND a drawback. Perks are earned through play and are purely advantageous.

Rest & Recovery

‚è± Minor Rest (1 Hour)

Spend 1 Stimpak to recover Endurance + Medicine rank in HP. Recover 2 Luck points. Momentum resets to 0. Cannot be done mid-combat.

üåô Major Rest (8 Hours)

Recover all HP. Refresh all Luck points. Treat one Minor Injury. Requires safe shelter and food/water. Radiaton and disease persist.

Hunger, Thirst & Sleep

The game tracks basic survival needs using the Deprivation mechanic. At the GM's discretion (typically every 24 hours without meeting a need), characters must make Endurance tests or suffer penalties:

Sleep+1 difficulty to Intelligence/Agility tests after 24hHallucinations (Complications more likely) after 48h

Radiation

The Fallout Wasteland is suffused with deadly radiation. Radiation is tracked as Rads ‚Äî a separate pool that gradually reduces your maximum HP.

Radiation Rules

For every 5 Rads accumulated, maximum HP decreases by 1 permanently (until cured). If your Rads equal your current maximum HP, you die. RadAway removes 5 Rads. Rad-X grants temporary Rad Resistance (+2 ER vs. radiation for 1 hour).

Disease

Exposure to contaminated water, disease-carrying creatures, or filthy environments can infect characters. Each disease has a Difficulty to resist (Endurance test when exposed) and a set of ongoing penalties until treated with Medicine or an Antibiotics item.

Crafting & Repair

The Wasteland is defined by making do with what you have. The Repair skill allows characters to fix broken gear, while Science enables crafting advanced modifications.

Weapon Maintenance

Weapons degrade after heavy use. A degraded weapon generates Complications on a 19‚Äì20 instead of just 20. A Repair test (Difficulty based on weapon complexity) restores normal function. Requires spare parts.

Crafting New Items

Craft weapons, food, chems, and explosives with appropriate skill tests. The GM sets Difficulty (usually 2‚Äì3), and you need the right components. Extra Momentum successes improve quality or yield multiple items.

Companions

Companions are NPCs who travel with the party. Each has their own S.P.E.C.I.A.L. stats, Skills, and unique Perks. On a player's turn, they may use their Major Action to command a companion (Simple order: no roll; Complex order: Charisma + Persuasion test, D1).

Companion Loyalty

Major decisions ‚Äî especially morally significant ones ‚Äî affect companion loyalty. Low loyalty companions may refuse orders, leave the party, or even turn hostile. Gifts, completing companion quests, and respecting their values all build loyalty.

Caps & Economy

Bottle caps are the currency of the Wasteland. The Barter skill governs trade, and the GM sets fair prices. Extra successes on a Barter test can lower prices or secure better deals. Some items are also traded in Junk ‚Äî raw materials used for crafting.
